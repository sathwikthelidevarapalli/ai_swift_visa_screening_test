Country: United States
Entry Category: Immigration Relief in Emergencies or Unforeseen Circumstances

1. Overview  
The U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) provides discretionary immigration relief measures for individuals affected by emergencies or unforeseen circumstances such as natural disasters, armed conflict, civil unrest, or large-scale disruptions.  

These measures are designed to prevent unfair immigration consequences for individuals who are unable to comply with normal immigration procedures due to circumstances beyond their control. Relief is temporary, situation-specific, and granted on a **case-by-case basis**.

2. Basic Eligibility Requirements  
- The applicant must have been **directly affected** by an emergency, disaster, or unforeseen circumstance (e.g., hurricanes, earthquakes, wars, or public health emergencies).  
- The applicant must **clearly demonstrate** how the emergency impacted their immigration situation — for example, by causing missed appointments, delayed filings, inability to depart the U.S., or loss of documentation.  
- The applicant must have an **active or pending immigration application or petition** with USCIS, or a need for immediate immigration action.  
- The applicant must **not have an available visa or lawful alternative** to resolve their situation, or must show that normal procedures were disrupted.  
- Relief is **discretionary**, and USCIS determines eligibility on a **case-by-case basis** depending on the nature and severity of the emergency.  
- Meeting eligibility criteria does **not guarantee approval**; each request is reviewed individually.  

3. Possible Forms of Relief  
Depending on the applicant’s circumstances, USCIS may offer:  
- **Extensions or changes of nonimmigrant status** for those unable to depart the U.S. before visa expiration.  
- **Re-parole** or extension of parole for those previously paroled into the U.S. under humanitarian or public interest grounds.  
- **Expedited processing** of petitions or applications related to urgent humanitarian need.  
- **Replacement of lost or damaged immigration documents** (e.g., Green Cards, Employment Authorization Documents).  
- **Flexibility for late filings**, missed appointments, or rescheduling of biometric or interview appointments.  
- **Temporary protection or deferred action** for individuals in life-threatening or emergency situations.  

4. Validity and Duration  
- Relief measures are typically **temporary** and tied to the duration of the specific emergency or crisis event.  
- USCIS determines the **duration and scope** of relief depending on the applicant’s situation and type of benefit requested.  
- Once the emergency ends, normal immigration rules and deadlines resume.  

5. Application Process  
1. The applicant or their representative must contact **USCIS** to explain the impact of the emergency.  
2. Requests may be made through a **written explanation** attached to a pending case, or by contacting the **USCIS Contact Center**.  
3. Supporting evidence (e.g., news reports, official notices, medical records, or other proof of hardship) must accompany the request.  
4. USCIS reviews the request and determines eligibility for discretionary relief.  
5. Approved applicants receive temporary immigration benefits or extensions consistent with USCIS guidance.  

6. Key Documents Required  
- Written explanation describing how the emergency or unforeseen circumstance affected the applicant’s immigration situation.  
- Proof of identity (passport, immigration document, or other official ID).  
- Evidence of the emergency or disaster (e.g., government declarations, media reports, or medical/emergency records).  
- Copies of pending immigration applications or petitions.  
- Proof of financial or humanitarian hardship caused by the emergency.  

7. Common Reasons for Ineligibility  
- Lack of evidence linking the applicant’s immigration difficulty to the emergency event.  
- Failure to provide supporting documentation or explanation.  
- Having alternative lawful immigration options or valid visa pathways.  
- Previous immigration violations, ineligibility, or inadmissibility grounds.  
- Discretionary denial by USCIS where the situation does not meet the urgency threshold.  

8. Legal References  
- Immigration and Nationality Act (INA) §212(d)(5) — Humanitarian Parole and Discretionary Authority.  
- 8 CFR §214.1(c)(4) — Extension or Change of Nonimmigrant Status.  
- USCIS Policy Manual, Volume 1, Part A, Chapter 5 — USCIS Discretionary Authority.  
- DHS and USCIS Emergency Response Guidance (case-specific determinations).  

9. Notes  
- Immigration relief measures are **temporary** and **do not create a new immigration status**.  
- Individuals granted relief may still be required to comply with other immigration or employment authorization requirements.  
- Requests should be submitted as soon as possible after the emergency occurs.  
- USCIS coordinates with FEMA, DHS, and other agencies when implementing relief measures during national emergencies.  
- All requests are **evaluated individually**, and outcomes depend on supporting evidence and humanitarian need
